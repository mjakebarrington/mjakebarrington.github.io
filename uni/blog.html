Self Teaching

In this day and age information is available virtually instantly at the click of a button.

Now, I find it prudent to defend self-teaching. In it's vastness the web offers many criticisms of the endeveor. In this section I will attempt to counter many such criticisms. I will make every attempt to be as objective as possible.

Lecture and text don't always parallel

This may be the easiest to debunk. While it's true that lectures often provide supplemental knowledge not available in a textbook or a course text, the internet offers vast amounts of recorded lectures. This is why in my curriculum I focus on a healthy mix of reading materials and recorded lectures. Nearly all computer science courses at MIT, for instance, have recorded lectures available at MIT OpenCourseWare. If a course doesn't happen to have recorded lectures available, another quality institution likely will. OpenCourser and FreeVideoLectures do a good job indexing many of them, among other sites. Yet others can be found with a simple Youtube search.

Grading yourself can lead to problems...

As the argument goes, we have innate biases for ourselves. One cannot fairly grade themself. This is why academic publications are peer reviewed, and essays/exams are graded by TAs and professors. There is some merit to self grading, but in the interest of modeling my curriculum after a 4 year college education, I will seek peer grading. Codementor comes to mind for seeking feedback on code as well as:

  • Stackexchange Code Review
  • Reddit Code Review (though not very active)
  • Codacy (for automated code testing)
  • Indorse

I recognize that codementor and indorse cost money, so they may not be available to everyone, but there are plenty of coding forums where folks are more than willing to take a look at your code.

As for subjects other than coding such as math, communication, core science:

Math

  • freeMATHhelp has a very active forum
  • My Math Forum has a less active forum, but covers more advanced math subjects.
  • Math 10 has a forum if the above two can't help.

Science

  • Science Forums covers Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Earth Science, and Ethics. It also has a forum for Math and a forum for Computer Science without code.

The Social Aspect

As with anything in life you get out what you put in. Yes, college is a great opportunity to make new friends and network. In my experience, however, this isn't necessarily something all college students look for.
